Systematic approach to any MSK lesion

  • Bone lesion — Enneking framework: (1) age; (2) location longitudinally (epi/meta/diaphyseal) + transversely (intramedullary/cortical/juxtacortical/soft tissue); (3) margins (sharply marginated geographic w/ sclerotic rim = benign; moth-eaten or permeative = aggressive); (4) zone of transition; (5) periosteal reaction (solid/continuous = benign; lamellated, sunburst, Codman triangle = aggressive); (6) matrix (osteoid = cloud/ivory; chondroid = rings & arcs; fibrous = ground glass); (7) bone destruction; (8) soft tissue mass; (9) multiplicity.
  • Soft tissue mass — checklist: size, depth (superficial vs deep to fascia), T1 signal vs muscle/fat, T2 + fat-sat behavior, margins (well-defined vs infiltrative), contrast enhancement (homogeneous vs nodular vs rim), neurovascular displacement vs invasion, calcifications on CT, adjacent bone involvement.
  • What to dictate: location, size in 3 dimensions, signal characteristics (T1/T2/fat-sat/contrast), margin, matrix/calcification, soft tissue/cortical involvement, periosteal reaction, ddx ranked by likelihood, recommendation (biopsy through compartment, refer to sarcoma center, follow-up interval).
  • Sequences to insist on: T1 (marrow + fat), T2-fs / STIR (edema + cystic), pre/post contrast, DWI (cellularity), GRE (susceptibility — hemosiderin in PVNS, calcium in some matrix).

Benign lesions where biopsy is contraindicated / unnecessary

LesionRecognition / why don't touch
Non-ossifying fibroma (NOF) / fibrous cortical defectEccentric, well-defined sclerotic margin, cortical-based, metaphysis of long bone, <30 yrs. Heals spontaneously.
Fibrous dysplasia (monostotic)"Ground glass" matrix, "shepherd's crook" deformity, expansile, age 5–30. Biopsy increases pathologic fracture risk.

Benign Bone Tumors

Osteoid osteomabone Radiopaedia
Anatomy / location
  • Adolescents / young adults (5–25 yrs); M > F
  • Cortical, metaphysis or diaphysis of long bone; posterior elements of spine
  • <2 cm nidus; if >2 cm = osteoblastoma
MRI fingerprint
  • Small T2-bright nidus (~1 cm) within cortex; surrounding T1-low, T2-bright marrow + soft tissue edema
  • Intense enhancement of nidus
  • CT shows nidus + central calcification
Numbers / clinical
  • Nidus <2 cm; classic nocturnal pain relieved by NSAIDs
  • Scoliosis if posterior spine — concave to lesion
Dictation

F: Cortical-based lesion of the [proximal femoral metaphysis] with a 7-mm nidus showing T2 hyperintensity, enhancement, and surrounding marrow + soft tissue edema. Adjacent reactive cortical thickening. No aggressive periosteal reaction.

I: Findings characteristic of osteoid osteoma; thin-section CT and/or correlation with NSAID-responsive nocturnal pain is recommended. Suitable for percutaneous radiofrequency ablation.

DDx mimics
  • Osteoblastoma (nidus >2 cm)
  • Brodie abscess (penumbra sign; rim enhancement)
  • Stress fracture (linear sclerosis, no central nidus)
  • Cortical desmoid (typical site)
Tips
  • Surrounding edema is often dramatic and may obscure the nidus on MRI alone — CT is the workhorse.
  • RFA is treatment of choice (except spine near cord).
Enchondromabone Enchondroma
Anatomy / location
  • Intramedullary cartilage; most common bone tumor of the hand (proximal phalanges)
  • Long bones: metaphysis/diaphysis (humerus, femur)
MRI fingerprint
  • Lobulated T1-low, T2-very-bright hyaline cartilage
  • "Rings and arcs" enhancement of cartilage matrix
  • CT shows characteristic chondroid mineralization
Numbers / concerning features
  • Size >5 cm, endosteal scalloping >2/3 cortex, cortical destruction, soft tissue mass, marked pain, growth on serial imaging — suspect chondrosarcoma
Dictation

F: Lobulated intramedullary T1 hypointense, T2 hyperintense lesion in the [distal femoral metadiaphysis] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] mm with internal chondroid mineralization on CT, mild endosteal scalloping (≤2/3 cortex), and lobulated peripheral enhancement. No cortical destruction or soft tissue mass.

I: Enchondroma without aggressive features. Recommend [no follow-up / baseline radiograph in 12 months given size] to ensure stability.

DDx mimics
  • Atypical cartilaginous tumor (ACT) / low-grade chondrosarcoma (endosteal scalloping >2/3, pain, growth)
  • Bone infarct (serpentine peripheral T2 dark rim, no chondroid matrix)
Tips
  • Hand enchondromas can be more aggressive-looking and still benign; long-bone enchondromas with growth/pain → workup for chondrosarcoma.
  • Ollier disease (multiple enchondromas) + soft tissue hemangiomas = Maffucci syndrome.
Osteochondroma (exostosis)bone Osteochondroma
Anatomy
  • Bony excrescence with cartilage cap; pedunculated (stalk) or sessile
  • Continuity of cortex + medullary space with parent bone — diagnostic feature
MRI fingerprint
  • Cartilage cap T2 hyperintense; measure perpendicular thickness
  • Continuous cortex/medulla with host bone
Concerning features
  • Cartilage cap thickness: <1 cm benign in adults; >1.5–2 cm in adults raises concern for chondrosarcoma; in skeletally immature normal cap can be 2–3 cm
  • Growth after physeal closure
  • New pain or soft tissue mass
Dictation

F: Pedunculated bony excrescence arising from the [posteromedial distal femoral metaphysis], with continuous cortex and marrow with the parent bone and a [X] mm thick cartilaginous cap on T2-fs. No soft tissue mass or aggressive features.

I: Osteochondroma without concerning features.

DDx mimics
  • Secondary chondrosarcoma (cap >1.5–2 cm in adult)
  • Subungual exostosis (foot)
  • BPOP (Nora lesion)
  • Soft tissue mass overlying a stable bony exostosis
Tips
  • Hereditary multiple exostoses (HME) — autosomal dominant; lifetime malignant transformation risk ~1–5%.
  • Always measure cartilage cap.
Aneurysmal bone cyst (ABC)bone ABC
Anatomy
  • Adolescents (<20 yrs); eccentric metaphyseal in long bones; posterior elements of spine
  • Often secondary to another lesion — GCT, chondroblastoma, fibrous dysplasia
MRI fingerprint
  • Eccentric expansile lesion; multiple fluid-fluid levels on T2 from sedimented blood
  • Internal septations; rim + septal enhancement; NO thick nodular soft tissue mass (that's telangiectatic OS)
Dictation

F: Eccentric expansile lesion of the [proximal tibial metaphysis] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with multiple fluid-fluid levels and thin internal septations. Cortical expansion and thinning without cortical destruction or solid enhancing soft tissue mass. No aggressive periosteal reaction.

I: Aneurysmal bone cyst. Recommend correlation to exclude an underlying primary lesion (e.g. GCT, chondroblastoma, fibrous dysplasia) and biopsy if any concerning solid component.

DDx mimics
  • Telangiectatic osteosarcoma — nodular enhancing components, aggressive periosteum (CRITICAL not to miss)
  • Giant cell tumor w/ secondary ABC
  • Chondroblastoma w/ secondary ABC
  • Simple bone cyst (no fluid levels, central, fallen-fragment sign)
Tips
  • Always look on post-contrast for nodular soft tissue enhancement — distinguishes ABC from telangiectatic OS.
  • Always search for an underlying primary lesion.
Giant cell tumor of bone (GCT)bone GCT
Anatomy
  • Age 20–40, F > M slightly
  • Eccentric, epiphyseal-metaphyseal, extends to subarticular cortex
  • Most common: distal femur, proximal tibia, distal radius ("around the knee")
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 + T2 intermediate-to-low signal (hemosiderin)
  • Solid enhancement; may have secondary ABC component with fluid-fluid levels
  • Extends to subchondral bone — characteristic
Dictation

F: Eccentric expansile lesion of the [distal femoral epiphysis–metaphysis] extending to the subchondral cortex, measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm, with T1/T2 intermediate signal, cortical thinning, and solid enhancement. [Secondary aneurysmal bone cyst component with fluid-fluid levels.] No soft tissue mass or aggressive periosteal reaction.

I: Lesion most consistent with giant cell tumor (Campanacci grade [I/II/III]). Biopsy recommended.

DDx mimics
  • ABC (younger, no solid enhancement)
  • Chondroblastoma (skeletally immature)
  • Brown tumor of hyperparathyroidism
  • Subchondral cyst (geode)
  • Telangiectatic osteosarcoma (if heterogeneous + aggressive features)
Tips
  • GCT is locally aggressive — Campanacci I (well-defined), II (cortical thinning), III (cortical destruction, soft tissue extension).
  • 3% can metastasize to lungs despite benign histology — recommend chest imaging.
Non-ossifying fibroma (NOF) / fibrous cortical defectbone NOF
Anatomy
  • <30 yrs; eccentric cortical-based lesion in metaphysis (distal femur, proximal tibia)
  • Sclerotic margin; usually <5 cm
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 + T2 low signal (fibrous tissue)
  • Sclerotic rim — well-defined
  • Heals over time (filled in with sclerosis)
Dictation

F: Eccentric cortical-based lytic lesion of the [distal femoral metaphysis] measuring [X] mm with sclerotic rim and T1/T2 low signal, characteristic of a non-ossifying fibroma. No aggressive features. Don't-touch lesion.

I: Non-ossifying fibroma; no further workup needed unless symptomatic.

Tips
  • Jaffe-Campanacci syndrome = multiple NOFs + café-au-lait macules.
  • Pathologic fracture risk if >50% bone width involved (Jeger-Brown criterion).
Fibrous dysplasiabone Fibrous dysplasia
Anatomy
  • Monostotic (most common) or polyostotic; McCune-Albright + Mazabraud syndromes
  • Ribs, femur, tibia, craniofacial
MRI fingerprint
  • "Ground glass" matrix on CT is classic
  • MRI: T1 + T2 variable; T2 low to intermediate (fibrous component), heterogeneous
  • Expansile but well-defined; "shepherd's crook" femur deformity
Dictation

F: Expansile intramedullary lesion of the [right femoral diaphysis] measuring [X] cm in length with ground-glass matrix on CT and heterogeneous T2 intermediate signal on MRI. Cortical thinning but intact; mild bowing deformity. No aggressive periosteal reaction or soft tissue extension.

I: Fibrous dysplasia [monostotic / polyostotic]. Don't-touch lesion unless growth or pathologic fracture.

DDx mimics
  • Adamantinoma (tibia) — looks similar but malignant
  • Osteofibrous dysplasia (tibial diaphysis)
  • Paget disease (older)
Tips
  • "Shepherd's crook" femoral deformity = classic.
  • Malignant transformation <1% (usually after radiation).
Simple (unicameral) bone cyst (SBC)bone SBC
Anatomy
  • Children + adolescents; central in proximal humerus or proximal femur metaphysis
MRI fingerprint
  • Central, well-defined, expansile fluid-filled lesion; T2 bright
  • "Fallen fragment sign" — pathologic fracture fragment that falls into dependent portion
Dictation

F: Central well-defined T1 low, T2 hyperintense expansile lesion of the [proximal humeral metaphysis] measuring [X] x [Y] cm with thin cortex and no soft tissue mass. [Pathologic fracture with fallen fragment sign.]

I: Simple (unicameral) bone cyst [± pathologic fracture].

Chondroblastomabone
Anatomy
  • Skeletally immature (10–20 yrs); epiphysis (or epiphyseal equivalent — apophysis)
  • Proximal humerus, distal femur, proximal tibia
MRI fingerprint
  • Lobulated lesion with T2 LOW signal (chondroid + matrix)
  • Surrounding marrow edema + joint effusion in 50%
  • Internal calcification on CT (chondroid matrix)
Dictation

F: Lobulated lytic lesion of the [proximal humeral epiphysis] measuring [X] mm with T2 hypointense matrix, internal calcifications on CT, and surrounding marrow edema and joint effusion.

I: Lesion most consistent with chondroblastoma in this skeletally immature patient.

DDx mimics
  • Eosinophilic granuloma
  • Subchondral cyst (no chondroid matrix)
  • Infection
  • GCT (after physeal closure)
Tips
  • "Chondroblastoma is a chondroblastoma until proven otherwise" in an epiphyseal lesion of a skeletally immature patient.

Malignant Bone Tumors

Osteosarcoma (conventional intramedullary)malignant Osteosarcoma
Anatomy
  • Bimodal age: 10–25 (primary), >60 (in Paget / post-radiation)
  • Metaphysis of long bones — distal femur (most common), proximal tibia, proximal humerus
MRI fingerprint
  • Aggressive intramedullary lesion w/ cortical destruction + soft tissue mass
  • Osteoid matrix ("cloud-like") on CT
  • Aggressive periosteal reaction: Codman triangle, sunburst, lamellated
  • Whole-bone STIR for skip lesions
Dictation

F: Aggressive intramedullary lesion of the [distal femoral metaphysis] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with cortical destruction, sunburst and Codman triangle periosteal reaction, ossified soft tissue mass, and intraosseous extent to within [N] cm of the joint line. No skip lesion on whole-bone STIR. [Lung nodules / pulmonary metastases on staging CT.]

I: Findings characteristic of osteosarcoma. Recommend referral to a sarcoma center and biopsy through a compartment that does not compromise limb-sparing surgery.

DDx mimics
  • Ewing sarcoma (diaphysis > metaphysis, age 5–20)
  • Stress fracture (linear)
  • Aggressive osteomyelitis
  • Telangiectatic osteosarcoma (fluid-fluid levels — see ABC ddx)
  • Periosteal / parosteal osteosarcoma (surface)
Tips
  • Always assess: skip lesions (whole-bone STIR), joint involvement, neurovascular bundle, growth-plate crossing, soft tissue compartments.
  • Refer to a sarcoma center BEFORE biopsy — biopsy tract is excised at surgery.
Ewing sarcomamalignant Ewing
Anatomy
  • Age 5–20; small round blue cell tumor
  • Diaphysis of long bones (~50%); flat bones (pelvis, ribs)
MRI fingerprint
  • Aggressive permeative bone destruction
  • Lamellated "onion-skin" periosteal reaction
  • Disproportionately LARGE soft tissue mass relative to bone destruction
  • T1 low, T2 intermediate, intense enhancement, restricted diffusion
Dictation

F: Permeative diaphyseal lesion of the [femoral diaphysis] with lamellated "onion-skin" periosteal reaction and disproportionately large extraosseous soft tissue mass measuring [X] x [Y] cm. T1 hypointense, T2 intermediate signal with intense enhancement and restricted diffusion. [Pulmonary / bone marrow metastases.]

I: Findings characteristic of Ewing sarcoma. Recommend sarcoma referral.

DDx mimics
  • Aggressive osteomyelitis (Brodie abscess; penumbra sign)
  • Eosinophilic granuloma (LCH)
  • Diaphyseal osteosarcoma
  • Lymphoma of bone (older patient)
Tips
  • Soft tissue mass often dwarfs bone destruction — characteristic.
  • Whole-body imaging for staging — Ewing metastasizes early.
Chondrosarcoma (and atypical cartilaginous tumor)malignant Chondrosarcoma
Anatomy
  • >40 yrs; pelvis, proximal femur/humerus, scapula; central or peripheral (from osteochondroma)
MRI fingerprint
  • Lobulated cartilage matrix — T2 bright, T1 low; "rings and arcs" enhancement
  • Aggressive features: cortical destruction, soft tissue mass, endosteal scalloping >2/3
  • WHO grade I (ACT in extremities), II, III; mesenchymal, dedifferentiated subtypes
Dictation

F: Lobulated intramedullary cartilage-matrix lesion of the [proximal femur] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with endosteal scalloping >2/3 cortical thickness, cortical destruction, an [N]-cm extraosseous soft tissue mass, and rings-and-arcs enhancement.

I: Aggressive cartilaginous tumor; chondrosarcoma highly suspected; recommend sarcoma center referral for biopsy and grading.

DDx mimics
  • Enchondroma (no aggressive features)
  • Bone infarct (serpentine peripheral rim, no chondroid matrix)
  • Chondroblastoma (different demographics, T2 low)
Tips
  • Differentiating ACT (grade I, extremity) from enchondroma is often based on aggressive features + pain + size.
Bone metastasesmalignant Skeletal mets
Anatomy
  • >40 yrs; multiple lytic / sclerotic lesions
  • Axial skeleton + proximal femur/humerus most common
  • Primaries: breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid, GI
MRI fingerprint
  • Multiple lesions; T1 low (replacing marrow), variable T2
  • Lytic mets: renal, thyroid, GI; sclerotic: prostate, breast (treated)
  • Use whole-body MRI / PET-CT for staging
Dictation

F: Multiple T1 hypointense, T2 hyperintense / heterogeneous marrow-replacing lesions within the [vertebrae, pelvis, and proximal femur], measuring up to [X] cm, with patchy enhancement. [Soft tissue extension into spinal canal / cortical destruction with pathologic fracture risk.]

I: Multifocal marrow-replacing lesions consistent with osseous metastatic disease. [Recommend whole-body PET-CT.]

Tips
  • State whether lesions are lytic vs sclerotic — narrows primary search.
  • Comment on impending pathologic fracture (Mirels score) for weight-bearing bones.
Multiple myelomamalignant
Anatomy
  • >50 yrs; multiple lytic "punched-out" lesions
  • Skull, spine, pelvis, proximal long bones
MRI fingerprint
  • Diffuse marrow infiltration: T1 marrow signal lower than disc/muscle = abnormal
  • Variegated / "salt-and-pepper" pattern
  • Whole-body MRI is the standard for myeloma staging
Dictation

F: Multiple T1 hypointense marrow-replacing lesions of the axial skeleton and proximal extremities with [diffuse / variegated / focal nodular] pattern of marrow involvement. [Lytic lesions with cortical breakthrough; soft tissue mass; pathologic fracture.]

I: Imaging findings compatible with multiple myeloma; recommend correlation with SPEP / urine BJP / serum free light chains and whole-body MRI for full staging.

Tips
  • "Mini-brain" appearance of plasmacytoma in vertebra.
  • Bone scan is INSENSITIVE for myeloma — use MRI or low-dose CT.

Benign Soft Tissue Tumors

Lipoma vs atypical lipomatous tumor / well-differentiated liposarcomasoft tissue ALT/WDLPS
Anatomy
  • Lipoma: superficial > deep; often subcutaneous
  • ALT/WDLPS: extremity, retroperitoneum, mediastinum; deeper, larger
MRI fingerprint
  • Lipoma: matches fat on T1 + T2; suppresses completely on fat-sat; thin (<2 mm) septa, no nodular enhancement
  • ALT/WDLPS concerning features: thick septations (>2 mm), nodular non-fatty soft tissue, contrast enhancement, size >10 cm, deep location (especially retroperitoneum/extremity), age >50
Dictation

F: Well-defined T1 hyperintense, T2 hyperintense [fat-density] lesion of the [thigh] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm, suppressing completely on fat-saturated sequences. Thin (<2 mm) septations without nodular soft tissue or contrast enhancement.

I: Simple lipoma without imaging features suspicious for atypical lipomatous tumor.

DDx mimics
  • Lipoma arborescens (synovial frondlike fat) — joint-based, classic suprapatellar pouch
  • Hibernoma (brown fat — slightly more T2 hyperintense)
  • Myxoid liposarcoma (different signal — T2 bright, fat-suppressing only in parts)
Tips
  • Any deep, large (>5 cm) "fatty mass" with non-adipose nodular enhancement = ALT/WDLPS until proven otherwise → refer.
Schwannoma + neurofibroma (peripheral nerve sheath tumors)soft tissue Schwannoma
Anatomy
  • Schwannoma — eccentric to nerve (nerve passes around mass), often encapsulated, sporadic; schwannomatosis
  • Neurofibroma — fusiform, intermingles with nerve fascicles; sporadic or NF1
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 isointense to muscle; T2 bright
  • "Target sign" on T2-fs (central low + peripheral high)
  • "Split fat" sign — fascicles displaced not surrounded (NF)
  • "Tail sign" / fascicular continuity with nerve
  • Intense enhancement
Dictation

F: Well-defined fusiform T1 isointense, T2 hyperintense mass measuring [X] x [Y] cm along the course of the [posterior tibial nerve] in the [tarsal tunnel], with target sign on T2-fs and split-fat sign. Intense enhancement. The nerve is contiguous with the mass with "tail sign". No aggressive features.

I: Peripheral nerve sheath tumor (likely schwannoma vs neurofibroma) along the [posterior tibial nerve]. Recommend referral for biopsy or surgical planning.

DDx mimics
  • Ganglion cyst (no enhancement, no target sign)
  • Soft tissue myxoma (no fascicular continuity)
  • MPNST (in NF1) — large, heterogeneous, aggressive features
Tips
  • "Tail sign" + target sign = strong evidence for nerve sheath tumor.
  • In NF1, any rapidly enlarging or painful neurofibroma → suspect malignant transformation to MPNST.
Tenosynovial giant cell tumor (TGCT) — diffuse PVNS / localized GCT-TSsynovial PVNS
Anatomy
  • Diffuse (formerly PVNS): knee > hip > ankle > shoulder
  • Localized (GCT of tendon sheath): hand most common, single nodule
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 + T2 LOW signal hemosiderin-containing nodular synovium
  • Blooming on GRE / SWI — diagnostic
  • Joint effusion + marginal erosions
Dictation

F: Diffuse low-T1, low-T2 nodular synovial thickening of the [right knee] with prominent blooming on GRE/SWI and adjacent marginal erosions of the intercondylar notch. Joint effusion.

I: Diffuse tenosynovial giant cell tumor (PVNS) of the [right knee].

Tips
  • Always use GRE/SWI when nodular dark synovium is suspected.
  • Localized GCT-TS in fingers vs glomus tumor (subungual painful).
Hemangioma (soft tissue)soft tissue
Anatomy
  • Common; superficial or deep extremities; muscle or subcutaneous
  • Phleboliths on radiograph / CT
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 intermediate w/ interspersed fat; T2 very bright "bag of grapes"
  • Multiple serpiginous vessels; phleboliths as round T2-dark foci
  • Marked enhancement
Dictation

F: Lobulated T1 intermediate, T2 hyperintense lesion within the [vastus lateralis] measuring [X] x [Y] cm with serpiginous internal vessels, several small T2-dark phleboliths, and marked enhancement. No aggressive features.

I: Intramuscular hemangioma.

Desmoid tumor (aggressive fibromatosis)soft tissue Desmoid
Anatomy
  • Locally aggressive fibroblastic tumor — no metastases but high recurrence
  • Extremity, abdominal wall (esp. postpartum), intra-abdominal (FAP / Gardner)
MRI fingerprint
  • Variable T1; T2 dark bands within heterogeneous mass (collagenous component)
  • Infiltrative margins; intense enhancement
  • "Staghorn / dural tail-like" infiltration along fascial planes
Dictation

F: Infiltrative soft tissue mass within the [anterior abdominal wall] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with T2 intermediate-to-hyperintense signal and interspersed T2 hypointense collagenous bands. Tongue-like extension along fascial planes. Marked enhancement.

I: Findings characteristic of desmoid tumor (aggressive fibromatosis); recommend sarcoma referral.

Tips
  • Pediatric desmoid in extremity may mimic sarcoma — biopsy.
  • T2 dark bands = collagenous fibrous tissue.

Malignant Soft Tissue Tumors

Synovial sarcomamalignant Synovial sarcoma
Anatomy
  • Young adults (15–40); near (NOT IN) joints — most often around knee, ankle, foot
  • NOT synovial in origin despite the name
MRI fingerprint
  • "Triple sign" on T2 = bright (cystic/necrosis), intermediate (cellular), dark (hemorrhage/fibrosis)
  • Fluid-fluid levels in ~10–25%
  • Calcifications on CT in ~30%
  • Often slow-growing → can be misread as benign
Dictation

F: Well-defined heterogeneous soft tissue mass adjacent to the [posterior knee joint] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with the classic "triple sign" of bright, intermediate, and dark T2 components, internal fluid-fluid levels, and peripheral calcifications. Marked enhancement.

I: Findings strongly suggestive of synovial sarcoma. Recommend immediate sarcoma center referral.

DDx mimics
  • Soft tissue hematoma — history of trauma, evolves over time
  • Hemangioma w/ phleboliths
  • Periarticular ganglion
Tips
  • Any heterogeneous para-articular mass >5 cm in a young adult — biopsy before assuming benign.
Undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (formerly MFH)malignant
Anatomy
  • Most common soft tissue sarcoma in older adults (50–80 yrs)
  • Extremity (esp. thigh), retroperitoneum
MRI fingerprint
  • Large, heterogeneous deep soft tissue mass
  • T1 intermediate, T2 heterogeneous + necrosis
  • Intense heterogeneous enhancement; central necrosis
Dictation

F: Large heterogeneous deep soft tissue mass within the [posterior compartment of the right thigh] measuring [X] x [Y] x [Z] cm with internal necrosis, T2 heterogeneous signal, and heterogeneous enhancement. [Adjacent neurovascular bundle displacement / muscle invasion.]

I: Large deep soft tissue mass concerning for high-grade sarcoma (undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma / dedifferentiated liposarcoma in ddx). Recommend sarcoma referral before biopsy.

Tips
  • Any deep soft tissue mass >5 cm in older adult is sarcoma until proven otherwise — refer.
Malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST)malignant
Anatomy
  • 50% arise in NF1 (lifetime risk ~10%)
  • Trunk + extremities along major nerves
MRI fingerprint
  • Large (>5 cm), heterogeneous, infiltrative margins
  • Internal necrosis + hemorrhage
  • Heterogeneous enhancement; ill-defined borders
  • Loss of "target sign" of benign nerve tumor
Dictation

F: Large heterogeneous nerve-associated mass along the [sciatic nerve] measuring [X] cm with internal necrosis, hemorrhage, infiltrative margins, and heterogeneous enhancement. Loss of normal "target sign". [Known NF1.]

I: Findings concerning for malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor in the setting of NF1; recommend urgent sarcoma referral.

Tips
  • In NF1, rapidly enlarging + painful neurofibroma → MPNST until proven otherwise.
  • PET-CT — high FDG uptake favors MPNST over benign neurofibroma.
Myxoid liposarcomamalignant
Anatomy
  • Most common soft tissue sarcoma in young adults; thigh most common
MRI fingerprint
  • T1 LOW (mimics water, dark), T2 BRIGHT (fluid-like)
  • BUT enhances avidly — NOT a cyst
  • Small focal fat-containing components (look for tiny fat foci that suppress)
Dictation

F: Lobulated T1 hypointense, T2 hyperintense soft tissue mass within the [posterior thigh] measuring [X] x [Y] cm with small interspersed fat foci that suppress on STIR. Marked heterogeneous enhancement throughout the lesion.

I: Lesion suspicious for myxoid liposarcoma (mass with fluid-like signal but solid enhancement). Recommend sarcoma referral.

DDx mimics
  • Cyst / ganglion (no enhancement!)
  • Myxoma (no fat foci; less enhancement)
  • Schwannoma
Tips
  • "Cyst that enhances" — biggest pitfall; always look at post-contrast to rule out solid mass.

Tumor Mimics

Common non-neoplastic lesions mistaken for tumors

MimicKey feature
Stress fractureLinear T1 hypointensity perpendicular to cortex + surrounding edema; specific bone (e.g. tibial shaft in runner).
Brodie abscessPenumbra sign (T1 bright rim); peripheral rim enhancement around T2 bright fluid.
Bone infarctGeographic lesion with serpentine T2 hyperintense rim ("double-line" sign); steroid use, sickle cell, alcohol.
Pseudotumor of hemophiliaRecurrent intramuscular / intraosseous hematoma in hemophilic patient; heterogeneous w/ rim enhancement.
PseudoaneurysmPulsatile mass; Doppler / contrast confirms vascular origin.
Myositis ossificans (early)Trauma history; immature peripheral ossification w/ edema; can mimic osteosarcoma in first weeks. Mature lesion has zonal phenomenon.
