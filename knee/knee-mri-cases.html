Meniscus

Case 1 — Medial meniscus posterior horn horizontal/oblique tear

Clinical: 54-year-old man, gradual medial joint-line pain after squatting at work, no acute injury.

F: On sagittal and coronal PD fat-sat images there is a horizontal-oblique high-signal line within the posterior horn of the medial meniscus reaching the inferior articular surface, measuring approximately 11 mm in AP extent. A small associated parameniscal cyst is seen tracking medially measuring 8 x 5 mm. No displaced fragment. Mild adjacent grade 2 chondral signal change over the medial femoral condyle without full-thickness loss. The ACL, PCL, and collateral ligaments are intact. No significant joint effusion.

I: Degenerative horizontal-oblique tear of the medial meniscus posterior horn with small parameniscal cyst.

Case 2 — Radial tear of the medial meniscus

Clinical: 47-year-old woman, twisting injury stepping off a curb, mechanical medial pain.

F: A vertically oriented full-thickness cleft perpendicular to the long axis of the medial meniscus is identified at the junction of the body and posterior horn, producing the truncated-triangle and ghost meniscus signs on consecutive sagittal images. The radial defect spans the full 9 mm meniscal width. Early 2 mm meniscal extrusion at the medial margin. No bucket-handle displacement. Cruciate and collateral ligaments intact. Trace effusion.

I: Full-thickness radial tear of the medial meniscus body–posterior horn junction.

Case 3 — Medial meniscus posterior root tear with extrusion

Clinical: 61-year-old woman, felt a pop rising from a chair, acute medial pain.

F: There is a radial-type defect at the posterior root attachment of the medial meniscus with a fluid-filled cleft and the ghost meniscus sign at the root on sagittal images. The meniscal body is extruded 5 mm beyond the medial tibial margin on the coronal sequence. Associated subchondral marrow edema within the medial femoral condyle and medial tibial plateau without discrete fracture line. Mild medial compartment cartilage thinning. ACL intact.

I: Medial meniscus posterior root tear with significant meniscal extrusion, functionally equivalent to a complete tear and predisposing to accelerated medial compartment degeneration.

Case 4 — Bucket-handle tear with displaced fragment and double-PCL sign

Clinical: 23-year-old male soccer player, locked knee unable to fully extend after a tackle.

F: The medial meniscus is foreshortened with absence of the normal bow-tie configuration on peripheral sagittal images. A displaced meniscal fragment lies within the intercondylar notch anterior and inferior to the PCL, producing the double-PCL sign on the sagittal sequence and the fragment-in-notch appearance on coronal images. The donor posterior horn is diminutive. Moderate joint effusion. ACL intact.

I: Displaced bucket-handle tear of the medial meniscus with intercondylar fragment (double-PCL sign), correlating with the locked knee.

Case 5 — Lateral meniscus tear

Clinical: 29-year-old man, pivoting basketball injury, lateral joint-line tenderness.

F: Vertical-longitudinal increased signal reaching the inferior surface of the posterior horn of the lateral meniscus measuring 7 mm, situated just lateral to the popliteus tendon hiatus (a normal structure, not to be mistaken for tear). No displaced fragment and no double-PCL sign. The lateral meniscus body and anterior horn are normal. Small reactive lateral compartment effusion. ACL intact.

I: Vertical-longitudinal tear of the lateral meniscus posterior horn.

Case 6 — Discoid lateral meniscus with tear

Clinical: 16-year-old girl, recurrent lateral clicking and intermittent pain, no major trauma.

F: The lateral meniscus demonstrates a continuous bow-tie configuration across more than three contiguous 4 mm sagittal slices and a widened transverse diameter exceeding 15 mm on coronal images, consistent with a discoid lateral meniscus (complete type). Within the thickened meniscus there is horizontal intrasubstance high signal reaching the inferior surface indicating a degenerative tear. No displacement. Cruciates intact.

I: Complete discoid lateral meniscus with a horizontal tear.

Case 7 — Degenerative meniscal maceration

Clinical: 68-year-old man, chronic medial pain, prior known osteoarthritis.

F: The medial meniscus posterior horn and body are diffusely irregular, blunted, and macerated with complex high signal and loss of the normal triangular morphology; no discrete reparable tear plane. Meniscal extrusion measures 4 mm medially. Background medial compartment full-thickness cartilage loss and subchondral cystic change. Marginal osteophytes. Cruciates intact.

I: Degenerative maceration of the medial meniscus in the setting of established medial compartment osteoarthritis.

Cruciate Ligaments

Case 8 — Acute complete ACL tear with pivot-shift contusions and Segond fracture

Clinical: 19-year-old female soccer player, non-contact pivot with a pop and immediate swelling.

F: The anterior cruciate ligament is discontinuous with absent fibers in the mid-substance, a wavy redundant distal stump, and increased T2 signal; the expected oblique orientation is lost. Characteristic pivot-shift bone contusions involve the lateral femoral condyle (sulcus terminalis) and posterolateral tibial plateau. A small cortical avulsion at the lateral tibial margin represents a Segond fracture, indicating anterolateral ligamentous injury. Anterior tibial translation and uncovering of the posterior horn lateral meniscus are noted. Large hemarthrosis.

I: Acute complete ACL tear with classic pivot-shift contusion pattern and Segond fracture.

Case 9 — ACL mucoid degeneration

Clinical: 58-year-old woman, posterior knee fullness and pain at terminal flexion, no instability.

F: The anterior cruciate ligament is diffusely thickened with ill-defined intermediate T2 signal interspersed between intact, taut fibers producing a celery-stalk appearance; the fibers remain continuous from origin to insertion. Adjacent intraosseous ganglion/mucoid cysts are present at the tibial insertion. No pivot-shift contusions and no anterior tibial translation. Menisci intact.

I: Mucoid degeneration of the ACL; no discrete tear or instability findings.

Case 10 — ACL reconstruction graft evaluation

Clinical: 31-year-old man, 2 years post ACL reconstruction, recurrent giving-way.

F: A bone–patellar tendon–bone ACL graft courses through the femoral and tibial tunnels. The intra-articular graft fibers are partially attenuated with intermediate signal and mild laxity; there is interval widening of the tibial tunnel and a focal full-thickness fiber discontinuity proximally suggesting partial graft tear. No cyclops lesion impinging on extension. Tunnel positions acceptable. Menisci intact.

I: Partial tear/attenuation of the ACL graft with tibial tunnel widening; clinical correlation for recurrent instability.

Case 11 — PCL tear

Clinical: 36-year-old man, dashboard injury in a motor-vehicle collision, posterior knee pain.

F: The posterior cruciate ligament is markedly thickened and edematous with intrasubstance high T2 signal and partial fiber disruption at the mid-substance; AP diameter measures 9 mm (upper limit normal ~7 mm). The femoral and tibial attachments remain intact, favoring an interstitial/partial tear rather than complete avulsion. Associated anterior tibial bone contusion. ACL intact. No posterior tibial avulsion fragment.

I: Acute high-grade partial (interstitial) PCL tear without bony avulsion.

Case 12 — O'Donoghue triad (ACL + MCL + medial meniscus)

Clinical: 25-year-old male skier, valgus + external rotation injury, immediate effusion.

F: Complete mid-substance tear of the anterior cruciate ligament with pivot-shift contusions of the lateral femoral condyle and posterolateral tibial plateau. The medial collateral ligament shows a high-grade sprain with periligamentous edema and partial proximal fiber disruption (grade 2–3). A peripheral vertical-longitudinal tear of the medial meniscus posterior horn is present at the meniscocapsular junction. Large hemarthrosis.

I: O'Donoghue unhappy triad: ACL tear, MCL injury, and medial meniscus tear.

Collaterals & PLC

Case 13 — MCL grade 2 sprain

Clinical: 21-year-old male football player, valgus blow to the lateral knee, medial tenderness.

F: The medial collateral ligament demonstrates periligamentous and intrasubstance edema with partial-thickness fiber disruption of the superficial component at its femoral origin; the deep fibers and overall ligament continuity are preserved. No complete discontinuity and no associated meniscal tear. Mild adjacent soft-tissue edema. ACL and PCL intact.

I: Grade 2 (partial) sprain of the superficial MCL at the femoral attachment.

Case 14 — LCL (fibular collateral ligament) tear

Clinical: 33-year-old man, varus injury with the foot planted, lateral instability.

F: The fibular (lateral) collateral ligament is thickened and discontinuous near its fibular head insertion with a lax, wavy distal stump and surrounding edema, consistent with a high-grade/complete tear. The biceps femoris insertion is also edematous. No fibular head avulsion fragment. Cruciates intact. Lateral meniscus intact.

I: High-grade tear of the fibular collateral ligament near the fibular insertion.

Case 15 — Posterolateral corner injury with arcuate sign

Clinical: 28-year-old man, hyperextension + varus injury, posterolateral pain and instability.

F: There is disruption of the posterolateral corner structures including the fibular collateral ligament, popliteus tendon, and popliteofibular ligament with diffuse periligamentous edema. A small avulsed cortical fragment at the fibular styloid represents the arcuate sign. Associated anteromedial femoral and tibial bone contusions reflect the hyperextension mechanism. Concurrent partial PCL injury is suspected.

I: Posterolateral corner injury with arcuate (fibular styloid avulsion) sign; assess for associated cruciate injury.

Extensor Mechanism & Patellofemoral

Case 16 — Patellar tendon partial tear

Clinical: 30-year-old recreational basketball player, anterior knee pain after jumping, palpable defect.

F: The patellar tendon is thickened at its proximal origin from the inferior patellar pole with intrasubstance high T2 signal and partial-thickness fiber disruption involving roughly 40% of the cross-section; the tendon remains in continuity. Adjacent Hoffa fat-pad edema and small inferior pole marrow edema. No complete rupture and no patella alta. No quadriceps tendon abnormality.

I: Proximal patellar tendon partial-thickness tear (jumper's knee), tendon in continuity.

Case 17 — Quadriceps tendon tear

Clinical: 63-year-old man, missed step on stairs, sudden inability to extend the knee.

F: The quadriceps tendon is completely disrupted approximately 1.5 cm above the superior patellar pole with a fluid-filled gap measuring 2.2 cm and retraction of the proximal tendon. There is patella baja from loss of the proximal tether and a moderate suprapatellar effusion with hemorrhage. The patellar tendon is intact. Mild background tendinosis at the torn margins.

I: Complete quadriceps tendon rupture with tendon gap and retraction; surgical repair indicated.

Case 18 — Acute lateral patellar dislocation with MPFL tear and bone-bruise pair

Clinical: 17-year-old girl, twisting non-contact injury during dance, sense of the kneecap "popping out."

F: Findings of transient lateral patellar dislocation with the characteristic bone-contusion pair: marrow edema involving the inferomedial patellar facet and the anterolateral femoral condyle. The medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) is disrupted at its patellar attachment with surrounding edema. A small osteochondral fragment (4 mm) is seen in the lateral gutter. Trochlear dysplasia and patella alta predispose. Large effusion/hemarthrosis.

I: Acute transient lateral patellar dislocation with MPFL tear, characteristic contusion pair, and a small osteochondral fragment.

Case 19 — Patellofemoral chondromalacia with maltracking

Clinical: 26-year-old woman, anterior knee pain with stairs and prolonged sitting, no trauma.

F: Axial PD fat-sat images show chondromalacia of the patellar median ridge and lateral facet with grade 3 fissuring and focal grade 4 full-thickness cartilage loss laterally. There is lateral patellar tilt (patellar tilt angle increased), a shallow trochlea, and an increased TT–TG distance of 19 mm indicating maltracking. Mild lateral retinacular thickening. No acute marrow edema.

I: Patellofemoral chondromalacia with lateral maltracking (increased TT–TG distance and patellar tilt).

Cartilage & Bone

Case 20 — Acute osteochondral injury

Clinical: 22-year-old man, acute impaction injury landing from a height, persistent pain.

F: A focal osteochondral injury of the lateral femoral condyle weight-bearing surface shows a 12 x 9 mm chondral fissure with underlying subchondral marrow edema and a partially detached osteochondral flap; fluid signal undermines the fragment indicating instability. A small intra-articular loose body is identified in the suprapatellar recess. Surrounding reactive effusion.

I: Acute unstable osteochondral injury of the lateral femoral condyle with a loose body.

Case 21 — Osteochondritis dissecans

Clinical: 15-year-old boy, chronic activity-related knee pain, occasional catching.

F: A well-corticated osteochondral lesion at the classic lateral aspect of the medial femoral condyle measures 15 x 12 mm. There is no high-signal fluid line fully encircling the fragment and the overlying cartilage is intact, indicating a stable lesion. No displaced fragment or loose body. Mild adjacent marrow edema. Physes open.

I: Stable osteochondritis dissecans of the medial femoral condyle (juvenile form).

Case 22 — SONK / subchondral insufficiency fracture

Clinical: 67-year-old woman, sudden spontaneous medial knee pain, no trauma, osteopenic.

F: The medial femoral condyle weight-bearing surface shows a subchondral low-signal fracture line paralleling the articular surface with extensive surrounding bone marrow edema and a focal subchondral T2-hypointense area, consistent with a subchondral insufficiency fracture (SONK pattern). Mild overlying chondral flattening without large defect. Adjacent medial meniscus posterior root tear with extrusion contributing to altered load. Joint effusion.

I: Subchondral insufficiency fracture of the medial femoral condyle (SONK), associated with medial meniscus root tear.

Case 23 — Tibial plateau contusion/fracture

Clinical: 44-year-old man, fall from a ladder onto a flexed knee, lateral pain and inability to bear weight.

F: The lateral tibial plateau demonstrates a depressed articular fracture with a vertically oriented split component and approximately 4 mm of articular depression (Schatzker II pattern); extensive surrounding marrow edema. A lipohemarthrosis with a fat–fluid level confirms intra-articular fracture. The lateral meniscus is displaced peripherally over the fracture margin. ACL intact.

I: Lateral tibial plateau split-depression fracture (Schatzker II) with lipohemarthrosis.

Case 24 — Tricompartmental osteoarthritis

Clinical: 71-year-old woman, chronic diffuse knee pain and stiffness, progressive over years.

F: There is tricompartmental osteoarthritis with full-thickness cartilage loss in the medial, lateral, and patellofemoral compartments, marginal osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis and cystic change, and degenerative maceration of both menisci with medial extrusion. Mild varus alignment. Moderate effusion with a Baker cyst. No acute fracture.

I: Advanced tricompartmental osteoarthritis with degenerative meniscal disease and a Baker cyst.

Other

Case 25 — Baker (popliteal) cyst

Clinical: 55-year-old woman, posterior knee fullness and occasional tightness.

F: A well-defined fluid signal collection arises between the medial head of gastrocnemius and the semimembranosus tendons, communicating with the joint, measuring 4.5 x 2.0 x 6.0 cm, consistent with a Baker cyst. Mild internal septation and a few loose bodies. No rupture or dissection into the calf and no surrounding edema to suggest leakage. Associated joint effusion and degenerative meniscal changes.

I: Uncomplicated Baker cyst arising at the gastrocnemius–semimembranosus bursa.

Case 26 — Pes anserine bursitis

Clinical: 59-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes, medial knee pain below the joint line.

F: A fluid collection deep to the conjoined pes anserine tendons (sartorius, gracilis, semitendinosus) along the anteromedial proximal tibia measures 3.2 x 1.1 cm with surrounding soft-tissue edema, consistent with pes anserine bursitis. No communication with the joint. The MCL is intact and uninvolved. Mild background medial compartment degenerative change.

I: Pes anserine bursitis.

Case 27 — Iliotibial band friction syndrome

Clinical: 34-year-old male long-distance runner, lateral knee pain at ~2 miles, worse downhill.

F: There is ill-defined edema in the soft tissues deep to the iliotibial band overlying the lateral femoral epicondyle, with the IT band itself mildly thickened. No discrete bursal fluid collection or intra-articular extension. The lateral meniscus, LCL, and popliteus are intact, excluding intra-articular causes of lateral pain.

I: Iliotibial band friction syndrome.

Case 28 — Chronic ACL-deficient knee

Clinical: 41-year-old man, remote untreated ACL injury, chronic instability and giving-way.

F: The anterior cruciate ligament is not identified in its expected course with only scarred residual fibers, and there is fixed anterior tibial translation with uncovering of the posterior horn of the lateral meniscus. Secondary degenerative changes include medial compartment cartilage loss and a chronic radial/degenerative tear of the medial meniscus. No acute marrow edema. Notch osteophytes are present.

I: Chronic ACL-deficient knee with secondary degenerative meniscal and chondral changes.

Case 29 — Medial meniscocapsular separation (ramp lesion)

Clinical: 20-year-old male footballer, ACL injury, persistent posteromedial pain.

F: At the posteromedial periphery there is fluid signal and disruption at the meniscocapsular junction of the medial meniscus posterior horn (ramp lesion) with adjacent posteromedial tibial marrow edema. This accompanies a complete ACL tear with typical pivot-shift contusions. The meniscal body itself is otherwise intact and non-displaced.

I: Medial meniscocapsular (ramp) lesion accompanying acute ACL tear; important to identify as it is easily missed and alters surgical management.

Case 30 — Hoffa fat-pad impingement syndrome

Clinical: 38-year-old woman, anterior knee pain with extension, no acute injury.

F: The superolateral aspect of the infrapatellar (Hoffa) fat pad demonstrates ill-defined edema-like high T2 signal and mild mass effect between the patellar tendon and lateral femoral condyle, consistent with Hoffa fat-pad impingement. The patellar tendon is intact without tear. No focal mass or hemorrhage. No effusion. Patellofemoral cartilage is preserved.

I: Superolateral Hoffa fat-pad impingement syndrome.
