Search pattern — by location

  • Lateral pain → common extensor origin (ECRB), LUCL on posterior coronals, radiocapitellar joint/plica, PIN at arcade of Frohse, capitellum for OCD.
  • Medial pain → common flexor origin, anterior band UCL at sublime tubercle (look for T-sign), ulnar nerve in cubital tunnel, posteromedial olecranon (valgus extension overload).
  • Anterior pain → distal biceps insertion at radial tuberosity (FABS view), brachialis, bicipitoradial bursitis, median nerve between PT heads.
  • Posterior pain → triceps insertion at olecranon, olecranon bursitis vs stress, posterior loose bodies in olecranon recess.
  • Always sweep the recesses — olecranon, coronoid, radiocapitellar — for loose bodies; check radial nerve in radial tunnel; check capitellum on T1 for marrow edema.

Modified Outerbridge Chondromalacia Grading — Copy-Ready

Use for any articular surface. Replace [surface/compartment]: radiocapitellar joint, capitellum, trochlea, ulnotrochlear joint, etc.

GradeMRI shorthand
0Normal cartilage.
1Signal heterogeneity/softening or swelling; surface intact.
2Superficial fissuring/fraying/partial-thickness defect <50% cartilage depth.
3Deep fissuring/partial-thickness defect >50% cartilage depth, not exposing bone.
4Full-thickness cartilage loss with exposed subchondral bone, often edema/cysts.
Grade 0 — normal cartilagecartilage grading
Anatomy

Hyaline cartilage on radial head, capitellum, trochlea, olecranon, coronoid.

Where on MRI

Coronal & sagittal PD-fs.

F: Articular cartilage of the [surface/compartment] is preserved in thickness and signal without focal fissuring, delamination, or subchondral marrow abnormality.

I: No chondral defect of the [surface/compartment].

Grade 1 — chondral softening / signal changecartilage
Tip

Subtle — confirm on two sequences. Don't over-call from a single fluid-sensitive image.

F: Mild focal cartilage signal heterogeneity/softening of the [surface/compartment] with preserved cartilage thickness and intact articular surface. No subchondral marrow edema.

I: Grade 1 chondromalacia of the [surface/compartment].

Grade 2 — superficial partial-thickness losscartilage
Tip

<50% depth. Often the earliest "real" defect on coronals.

F: Superficial chondral fissuring/fraying of the [surface/compartment] involving less than 50% of cartilage thickness. No full-thickness defect or subchondral marrow edema.

I: Grade 2 chondromalacia of the [surface/compartment].

Grade 3 — deep partial-thickness losscartilage
Tip

>50% depth, bone NOT exposed. Subchondral edema often appears here.

F: Deep partial-thickness chondral fissuring/defect of the [surface/compartment] involving greater than 50% of cartilage thickness without exposed subchondral bone. [Mild adjacent subchondral marrow edema.]

I: Grade 3 chondromalacia of the [surface/compartment].

Grade 4 — full-thickness defect, exposed bonecartilage
Tip

Measure the defect (mm). State if unstable flap or loose body present.

F: Full-thickness cartilage loss/fissuring of the [surface/compartment] measuring [X] x [Y] mm with exposed subchondral bone and [subchondral marrow edema/cystic change]. [Small unstable chondral flap/loose body if present.]

I: Grade 4 chondromalacia/full-thickness chondral defect of the [surface/compartment].

Tendons

Common extensor tendinosis (lateral epicondylitis)lateral MRI accuracyRadiopaedia
Anatomy
  • Origin: anterolateral facet of lateral epicondyle
  • Culprit: ECRB undersurface (deep fibers) almost always
  • Common extensor blends with RCL beneath it
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs lateral side, then sagittal for partial-thickness
  • Compare to contralateral side mentally — uniform low-signal triangle

F: Thickening and intermediate signal of the common extensor tendon origin with [low-grade partial-thickness interstitial tearing] and adjacent peritendinous edema.

I: Common extensor tendinosis/lateral epicondylitis [with low-grade partial tear].

DDx mimics
  • LUCL injury — look on posterior coronals
  • Radiocapitellar plica syndrome — plica >3 mm or covers >1/3 capitellum
  • Radial tunnel syndrome (PIN at arcade of Frohse) — denervation edema in supinator
  • Capitellar OCD — subchondral edema/cyst
  • Posterolateral rotatory instability
Tips
  • Always look at LUCL whenever you see lateral epicondylitis — adjacent partial-thickness LUCL injury changes management.
  • Reflex check capitellum cartilage on the same slice.
Common extensor high-grade / full-thickness tearlateral Radiopaedia
Anatomy
  • Same origin as above; tear typically at enthesis
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs — fluid-filled gap at lateral epicondyle
  • Measure retraction in mm

F: High-grade partial/full-thickness tear of the common extensor tendon origin from the lateral epicondyle with [X] mm retraction and surrounding soft tissue edema.

I: High-grade tear of the common extensor tendon origin.

DDx mimics
  • LUCL avulsion (often coexists — combined injury upgrades to instability)
  • Lateral epicondyle stress reaction
Tips
  • State LUCL status explicitly — surgeons need to know.
  • Look for retracted ligament stump / Bosworth-type avulsion fragment.
Common flexor tendinosis (medial epicondylitis / "golfer's elbow")medial MRI accuracyRadiopaedia
Anatomy
  • Origin: anterior facet of medial epicondyle
  • Pronator teres + FCR typically affected
  • Common flexor lies superficial to UCL
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs medial; check UCL on the SAME image

F: Thickening and intermediate signal of the common flexor tendon origin with mild peritendinous edema. No full-thickness tear.

I: Common flexor tendinosis/medial epicondylitis.

DDx mimics
  • UCL anterior band injury — often co-exists (especially throwers)
  • Ulnar neuritis / cubital tunnel syndrome
  • Snapping triceps (medial head)
  • In a skeletally immature: medial epicondyle apophyseal injury ("Little Leaguer's elbow")
Tips
  • Medial pain + edema at sublime tubercle = think distal UCL tear, not just flexor tendinosis.
  • Always report the ulnar nerve next to the medial side.
Distal biceps tendon tearanterior Radiopaedia
Anatomy
  • Insertion: radial tuberosity (footprint posterior)
  • Lacertus fibrosus = bicipital aponeurosis tethering tendon to forearm fascia; if intact, retraction is limited
Where on MRI
  • FABS view — Flexed elbow, ABducted shoulder, Supinated forearm — shows entire distal tendon in one long-axis plane
  • Axial T2-fs through antecubital fossa to grade thickness

F: Full-thickness tear of the distal biceps tendon from the radial tuberosity with [X] cm proximal retraction and fluid/hemorrhage along the bicipitoradial bursa. [Lacertus fibrosus intact/disrupted.]

I: Complete distal biceps tendon rupture with [X] cm retraction.

DDx mimics
  • Partial undersurface tear (radial-sided footprint) — needs FABS
  • Bicipitoradial bursitis ("cubital bursitis") — fluid without tendon discontinuity; mass effect on PIN
  • Lacertus fibrosus isolated tear
  • Brachialis strain (deep + medial to biceps)
Tips
  • State retraction in cm and lacertus status — surgeon will choose 1- vs 2-incision repair on these.
  • Fluid in bicipitoradial bursa without tendon defect ≠ tear; it can cause PIN palsy by mass effect.
Distal triceps tendon tearposterior Triceps review
Anatomy
  • Insertion: posterior superior olecranon
  • Three heads — superficial (long + lateral) and deep (medial); usually a thin superficial slip is the first to tear and retract while deep fibers stay intact
Where on MRI
  • Sagittal PD-fs posterior — measure thickness of intact fibers
  • Axial T2-fs — grade % cross-section involved

F: Partial/full-thickness tear of the distal triceps tendon at the olecranon insertion with surrounding posterior soft tissue edema and [olecranon enthesophyte/avulsion fragment].

I: [Partial/full-thickness] distal triceps tendon tear.

DDx mimics
  • Olecranon bursitis
  • Olecranon stress reaction / stress fracture (throwers, weightlifters)
  • Posteromedial impingement / valgus extension overload — posteromedial olecranon osteophyte
  • Snapping triceps (medial head dynamic dislocation)
Tips
  • Partial superficial tears commonly missed — comment specifically on % thickness involved.
  • Anabolic steroid users / fluoroquinolone users — be suspicious for spontaneous rupture.

Ligaments / Instability

UCL sprain (anterior band partial tear)medial UCL injury
Anatomy
  • Anterior band: medial epicondyle → sublime tubercle of coronoid
  • Primary valgus stabilizer at 30–120°
  • Length ~ 27 mm, thickness 4–5 mm normal
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs — uniform low-signal band
  • T-sign = contrast between distal UCL and sublime tubercle = partial undersurface tear (best on MR arthrogram)
  • Edema at sublime tubercle on T2-fs = strong clue even without arthrogram

F: Ulnar collateral ligament is thickened and edematous with partial fiber irregularity, greatest at the [proximal/distal] attachment. No complete discontinuity.

I: Grade [1/2] UCL sprain/partial tear.

DDx mimics
  • Common flexor tendinosis — superficial to UCL, often coexists
  • Ulnar neuritis — posterior to medial epicondyle
  • Posteromedial impingement
  • Medial epicondyle stress reaction
Tips
  • Throwers with medial pain → strongly consider MR arthrogram if conventional MRI shows borderline UCL.
  • Comment on chronicity — calcified ligament suggests chronic insult.
UCL complete tear (anterior band)medial UCL injury
Anatomy
  • Most tears at humeral or mid-substance; pure distal avulsion possible
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs — fluid-filled gap across entire ligament thickness
  • State location: humeral / mid-substance / ulnar

F: Complete discontinuity of the anterior band of the ulnar collateral ligament at the [humeral/ulnar] attachment with fluid-filled gap and medial soft tissue edema.

I: Complete tear of the anterior band UCL.

DDx mimics
  • Chronic high-grade partial — fibers attenuated but technically continuous
  • Medial epicondyle avulsion (skeletally immature)
Tips
  • State tear location — proximal humeral tears generally repair-eligible; mid-substance tears = Tommy John reconstruction territory.
  • Comment on coexisting flexor mass and ulnar nerve.
RCL / LUCL injury (lateral ligament complex)lateral Elbow instability
Anatomy
  • RCL: lateral epicondyle → annular ligament
  • LUCL: lateral epicondyle (deep/posterior to RCL) → supinator crest of ulna
  • LUCL is THE posterolateral rotatory stabilizer
Where on MRI
  • POSTERIOR coronal slices — LUCL runs obliquely behind the radial head
  • "Arcuate sign" — bone edema at supinator crest = LUCL avulsion
  • Often coexists with common extensor tear after lateral dislocation

F: Irregularity/discontinuity of the [radial collateral/lateral ulnar collateral] ligament complex with posterolateral capsular edema [and bone marrow edema at the supinator crest].

I: [RCL/LUCL] tear; correlate for posterolateral rotatory instability.

DDx mimics
  • Common extensor partial tear — sits superficial to RCL
  • Radiocapitellar plica — synovial fold mimics LUCL fiber irregularity
  • Posterolateral synovial recess fluid (normal variant)
Tips
  • Any patient with prior elbow dislocation → carefully evaluate LUCL on posterior coronals.
  • Combined LUCL + common extensor tear = "lateral collateral ligamentous complex" injury → instability.

Cartilage / Nerves / Loose Bodies

Capitellar osteochondral lesion (OCD)cartilage MRI staging
Anatomy
  • Capitellum = lateral half of distal humerus articular surface
  • Classic OCD site = anterolateral capitellum
  • Throwers, gymnasts; adolescents
Where on MRI
  • Coronal PD-fs + sagittal — measure lesion size and look for fluid undermining the fragment
  • T1 marrow signal — low = sclerosis; preserved = stable
  • Pseudodefect of capitellum (posterolateral, where capitellum ends abruptly) is NORMAL — do not call OCD

F: Osteochondral lesion of the capitellum measuring [X] mm with overlying chondral irregularity, subchondral marrow edema/cystic change, and [fluid undermining/loose body].

I: Capitellar osteochondral lesion, [stable/unstable].

DDx mimics
  • Panner's disease (younger child <10y) — diffuse capitellar marrow signal, self-limited
  • Pseudodefect of capitellum (normal variant, posterolateral)
  • Radiocapitellar OA
  • Stress reaction of capitellum
Tips
  • Stability features (any one = unstable): fluid line around fragment, cyst > 5 mm under it, defect >5 mm, displaced fragment.
  • Always search the radiocapitellar / coronoid / olecranon recesses for the loose body.
Ulnar neuritis / cubital tunnel syndromenerve Cubital tunnel
Anatomy
  • Cubital tunnel borders: medial epicondyle (lateral), olecranon (medial), Osborne ligament / cubital tunnel retinaculum (roof)
  • Distal to tunnel: nerve dives between two heads of FCU
Where on MRI
  • Axial T2-fs at and just above the medial epicondyle
  • Compare ulnar to median nerve — should be similar caliber/signal
  • Look for accessory anconeus epitrochlearis muscle replacing/joining Osborne ligament

F: Ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel is enlarged and T2 hyperintense with surrounding perineural edema. No compressive mass identified. [Anconeus epitrochlearis present.]

I: Ulnar neuritis/cubital tunnel syndrome pattern.

DDx mimics
  • Medial epicondylitis — superficial common flexor signal, not in tunnel
  • Anconeus epitrochlearis (accessory muscle) — known compressive variant
  • Subluxing ulnar nerve (dynamic — visible only on flexion imaging)
  • Ganglion / mass in tunnel
  • Post-traumatic scarring
Tips
  • State nerve caliber AND signal AND whether anconeus epitrochlearis is present — surgeon plans differently.
  • Denervation edema in FCU and FDP (ulnar half) confirms motor involvement.
Radial tunnel syndrome / PIN compressionnerve PIN syndrome
Anatomy
  • Radial nerve divides anterior to radiocapitellar joint into superficial (sensory) and PIN (motor)
  • PIN dives through supinator beneath the arcade of Frohse (fibrous edge of supinator)
Where on MRI
  • Axial T2-fs at radial neck
  • Denervation edema in supinator, EDC, ECU, APL, EPL, EPB

F: Mild T2 hyperintensity and minimal enlargement of the posterior interosseous nerve at the arcade of Frohse with [denervation edema of the supinator and superficial extensor compartment]. No discrete compressive mass.

I: Findings consistent with radial tunnel syndrome / PIN compression at the arcade of Frohse.

DDx mimics
  • Lateral epicondylitis — overlapping clinical picture, common confusion
  • Bicipitoradial bursitis pressing on PIN
  • Lipoma / ganglion in radial tunnel
Tips
  • Always check supinator muscle signal whenever lateral elbow pain — denervation pattern clinches PIN.
Synovitis / intra-articular loose bodycartilage Loose bodySynovial chondromatosis
Anatomy
  • Three recesses harbor loose bodies: coronoid (anterior), olecranon (posterior), radiocapitellar (lateral)
Where on MRI
  • Sagittal PD-fs — sweep all three recesses
  • Axial — radiocapitellar recess
  • T1 — calcified body = dark; cartilage body = intermediate

F: Small elbow effusion with synovial thickening and [X] mm intra-articular osteochondral body in the [olecranon/coronoid/radiocapitellar] recess.

I: Elbow synovitis with intra-articular loose body.

DDx mimics
  • Synovial chondromatosis — multiple uniform bodies
  • PVNS / TGCT — low signal nodular synovium, blooming on GRE
  • Inflammatory synovitis (RA) — pannus, marginal erosions
  • Posttraumatic intra-articular fragment
Tips
  • Number and size all loose bodies — surgeon plans portals around the largest.
  • Loose body without OA/OCD source → consider synovial chondromatosis.
Posteromedial impingement / valgus extension overloadposterior VEO syndrome
Anatomy
  • Repetitive valgus + extension (throwers) drives the posteromedial olecranon tip against the medial trochlea/olecranon fossa
Where on MRI
  • Sagittal + axial: posteromedial olecranon tip osteophyte ("kissing" lesion)
  • Trochlear chondrosis, marrow edema
  • Coexisting UCL injury and loose body in olecranon fossa

F: Posteromedial olecranon tip osteophyte with chondral irregularity of the opposing medial trochlea and posteromedial olecranon recess synovitis. [Adjacent loose body / UCL thickening.]

I: Posteromedial impingement / valgus extension overload syndrome.

DDx mimics
  • Olecranon stress fracture
  • Triceps insertional tendinosis
  • Loose body alone (without osteophyte)
Tips
  • Almost always coexists with UCL pathology — comment on both.
Radiocapitellar plica syndromelateral Elbow plica
Anatomy
  • Synovial fold along posterolateral radiocapitellar joint
  • Becomes symptomatic when thickened or large
Where on MRI
  • Coronal + axial PD-fs
  • Symptomatic threshold: >3 mm thickness or covers >1/3 capitellum

F: Thickened synovial fold along the posterolateral radiocapitellar joint measuring [X] mm with adjacent chondral irregularity of the capitellum.

I: Symptomatic radiocapitellar plica.

DDx mimics
  • LUCL fiber irregularity (deeper, more posterior)
  • Common extensor tear (superficial)
  • Loose body in radiocapitellar recess
Tips
  • Treat as additional ddx in any lateral elbow pain MRI; surgically resectable.
Bicipitoradial (cubital) bursitisanterior Bicipitoradial bursitis
Anatomy
  • Bursa between distal biceps tendon and radial tuberosity
  • Friction from supination/pronation
Where on MRI
  • Axial + sagittal — fluid collection between intact biceps tendon and radial tuberosity
  • Look for adjacent PIN — mass effect can cause PIN palsy

F: Fluid distention of the bicipitoradial bursa adjacent to the radial tuberosity, intact distal biceps tendon. [Mass effect on adjacent posterior interosseous nerve.]

I: Bicipitoradial bursitis without distal biceps tendon tear.

DDx mimics
  • Partial distal biceps tear (look closely at footprint)
  • Ganglion arising from proximal radioulnar joint
  • Soft tissue mass
Tips
  • Don't mistake bursal fluid for biceps tear — trace the tendon to its footprint.
  • If PIN denervation pattern → bursitis is the cause.

High-yield pitfalls / normal variants that mimic disease

  • Pseudodefect of the capitellum — posterolateral notch where capitellar cartilage ends; normal. Do NOT call OCD.
  • Pseudodefect of the trochlear groove — bare bone between the olecranon and coronoid facets; normal anatomy.
  • Anconeus epitrochlearis — accessory muscle replacing the cubital tunnel retinaculum (Osborne ligament); a common cause of ulnar neuritis. Identify and report it.
  • Cubital bursitis (bicipitoradial bursa fluid) — mimics partial distal biceps tear; can compress PIN.
  • Lacertus fibrosus rupture — isolated tear of the bicipital aponeurosis without biceps tendon tear; missed if you don't look for it.
  • Posterior coronal slices for LUCL — LUCL only seen on posterior coronals, never on the anterior images you instinctively scroll to.
  • Sublime tubercle edema = distal UCL avulsion until proven otherwise (especially throwers).
  • Magic-angle in distal biceps tendon at radial tuberosity on short-TE sequences — confirm signal abnormality on T2-fs/STIR.
  • Supinator muscle edema on a "lateral epicondylitis" MRI → suspect radial tunnel / PIN compression, not just tendinosis.
  • Radiocapitellar plica — synovial fold >3 mm or covering >1/3 capitellum is symptomatic, not incidental.
